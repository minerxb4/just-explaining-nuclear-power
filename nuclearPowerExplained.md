# How a Nuclear Power Plant Works

* Water from a lake or river goes into the cooling tower.
* That water is then pumped into a steam generator, which generates steam from the heat of the nuclear core (usually uranium).
* The heat is generated from fission chain reactions from neutrons and controlled by the control rods that absorb the neutrons and keep things from overheating.
* This core is surrounded by coolant, which is very good at transferring heat.
* It transfers that heat to the steam generator, which is a bunch of pipes filled with water from the intake.
* This water turns into steam from the heat generated by the nuclear core.
* To clarify, those big tower things are **NOT** the reactor; those are for cooling.
* The reactor is very well shielded with material that can block the harmful radiation particles.
* Back to the steam generator: the steam goes up and spins a turbine which is connected to a generator which produces power for the city.
* So basically, it's just a complicated steam engine that runs on nuclear fission reactions instead of burning coal.
* The reactor produces **A** **LOT** of heat, and a lot of it isn't able to be used at 100% efficiency.
* Therefore, the excess heated water is recondensed and then transferred to the cooling tower where it is sprayed out and turns back into harmless vapor that doesn't pollute anything.
* This all keeps the reactor cool, protects us from meltdown, and keeps us safe.
